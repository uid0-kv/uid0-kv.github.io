S-R Latches and Active Highs/Lows

2026-06-27 · Learning · MetaWriting

Motivation: When I was understanding this topic, I came across several resources and terminologies that mixed concepts with each other, making it difficult to comprehend the core logic. Terms like active low, active high, active high reset, active low enable and several others are fundamentally simple, but that's where I face the difficulty.

I was facing this confusion: why is NAND SR latch active-low when S and R are still called S and R and the circuit looks structurally similar to the NOR version.

This blog covers my understanding of that confusion and what I found as I resolved it.

S-R Latch

The S-R (Set-Reset) latch is one of the simplest sequential logic circuits and the most basic memory element in digital electronics. Unlike combinational circuits, whose outputs depend only on the current inputs, an S-R latch can store one bit of information by remembering its previous state. Many more complex memory elements, such as D latches and flip-flops, are derived from this fundamental circuit.

An S-R latch can be constructed using either NAND or NOR gates, both of which are universal gates capable of implementing any Boolean function. Each implementation has its own advantages and disadvantages. In practice, NAND-based implementations are more common because NAND gates are generally more efficient in many logic families. Older TTL devices often implemented NAND gates with better speed and drive characteristics than equivalent NOR gates, and modern CMOS standard-cell libraries also make extensive use of NAND gates. Consequently, many practical digital circuits are built around NAND-based latch implementations.

One important distinction between the two implementations is that a NAND-based S-R latch is an active-low circuit, whereas a NOR-based S-R latch is an active-high circuit. To understand what this means, we first need to understand the concepts of active-high and active-low signals.

Active-high and active-low describe which logic level causes a signal to perform its intended function. A signal is said to be active-high if its intended action occurs when the signal is at logic 1. Conversely, a signal is active-low if its intended action occurs when the signal is at logic 0.

Example:

Forget latches for a moment and consider a simple light switch controlling a light bulb. Let us associate the following logic values with the switch and bulb states:

Switch OFF ⇔ Logic 0      Switch ON ⇔ Logic 1

Bulb OFF ⇔ Logic 0      Bulb ON ⇔ Logic 1

Suppose the intended function of the switch is to turn the bulb on. The intended action occurs when the switch is in the ON position (logic 1). Therefore, this is an active-high control signal.

Now suppose we redefine the intended function to be turning the bulb off, while leaving the switch states unchanged. The intended action now occurs when the switch is OFF (logic 0). Under this convention, the same switch behaves as an active-low control signal.

The important observation is that nothing about the hardware has changed. Only our definition of the signal's intended function has changed. Whether a signal is active-high or active-low depends on:

  • the intended function of the signal,
  • which logic level (0 or 1) represents each state, and
  • the naming convention used for the signal (for example, RESET, RESET̅, or RESET_N).

Personally, I find it more useful to remember the behaviour of a circuit than to memorize every active-low naming convention. Once you begin working with more complex circuits, such as D latches and D flip-flops, understanding how the circuit operates becomes far more valuable than remembering whether a particular input is active-high or active-low. Nevertheless, these conventions become extremely important when reading datasheets, debugging digital circuits, or interpreting timing diagrams and waveform traces.

As you can see, considering S as our driving port and Q as our main output, we can have both Active High and Active Low implementations by just swapping the output port names. Given NAND is considered to be an Active Low implementation, we have the bottom right implementation for it. Similarly, for NOR we have the top left implementation.
